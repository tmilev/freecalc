\solution{\ref{problemFindCenterAndRadius2x^2+2y^2-x+2y=3}

The standard equation of a circle with center $(h,k)$ and radius $r$ is 
\[
(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2.
\] 
Our strategy consists in transforming the equation $2x^2+2y^2-x+2y=3$ into that form. This is done by completing the square, as we show below. We have included all computations with extreme detail. Such detail is not needed when solving test problems; students are encouraged to carry out some of the steps ``in the head''.

\[
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.7}
\begin{array}{rcll|l}
\displaystyle 2x^2+2y^2-x+2y&=&\displaystyle 3\\
\displaystyle x^2+y^2-\frac{1}{2}x+y&=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2} &&\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1}
\begin{array}{l}\text{Divide by }2 \\ \text{ to ensure }x^2\\ \text{ has coeff. }1\end{array}\\
\displaystyle x^2-\frac{1}{2}x + y^2+y&=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2} &&\text{group the }x\text{'s} \text{ and the }y\text{'s}\\
\displaystyle x^2-2\cdot \frac{1}{4} x+ y^2+2\cdot\frac{1}{2}y &=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2} && \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1}
\begin{array}{l}
\text{multiply and divide}\\
\text{the linear terms by }2
\end{array} \\
\displaystyle \underbrace{x^2-2\cdot {\color{blue}\frac{1}{4}} x +\left({\color{blue}\frac{1}{4}} \right)^2}_{\text{square}}-\left({\color{blue}\frac{1}{4}}\right)^2 +\underbrace{y^2+2\cdot {\color{red}\frac{1}{2}} y+\left({\color{red} \frac{1}{2}}\right)^2}_{\text{square}} -\left({\color{red} \frac{1}{2}}\right)^2 &=&\displaystyle  \frac{3}{2} && \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1}
\begin{array}{l} \text{Add and subtract } \\\text{appropriate constants }\\ \text{to facilitate completing the square} \end{array}\\
\displaystyle \left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 -\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 +\left(y+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 -\left(\frac{1}{2} \right)^2&=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2} && 
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1}
\begin{array}{l}
\text{Complete square using: } \\
(p\pm  q)^2=p^2\pm 2pq+q^2
\end{array}\\
\displaystyle \left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 +\left(y+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2&=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2}+\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 && \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1} \begin{array}{l}\text{transfer constants}\\ \text{to the other side}\end{array}\\
\displaystyle \left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 +\left(y-\left( -\frac{1}{2}\right)\right)^2&=&\displaystyle  \frac{3}{2}+\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2&& \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1} 
\begin{array}{l}
\text{Ensure squares} \\ 
\text{are of form}\\
(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2
\end{array}\\
\displaystyle \left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 +\left(y-\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right)^2 &=&\displaystyle \frac{3}{2}+\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{16}\\
&=&\displaystyle  \frac{24+4+1}{16}\\
&=&\displaystyle  \frac{29}{16}\\
\displaystyle \left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 +\left(y-\left(-\frac{1}{2} \right)\right)^2 &=&\displaystyle \left(\sqrt{\frac{29}{16}}\right)^2 && \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1}\begin{array}{l} 
\text{final step: rewrite constant }\\
\text{using } a=\left(\sqrt{a}\right)^2.
\end{array}
\end{array}
\]
The equation above is in the standard equation form for a circle $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$. It follows that the equation is of a circle with center $\left(\frac{1}{4},-\frac{1}{2} \right)$ and radius $\frac{\sqrt{29}}{4}$.
