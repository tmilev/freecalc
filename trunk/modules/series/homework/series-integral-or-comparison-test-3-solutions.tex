\solution{\ref{problemConvergenceint_10^inftyx/(xlnx)dx}.
\[
\begin{array}{rcl}
\displaystyle \int_{10}^{\infty}\frac{1}{x\ln x}\diff x&=&\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} \int_{10}^{t}\frac{1}{x\ln x}\diff x\\
&=&\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} \int_{10}^{t}\frac{1}{\ln x}\diff(\ln x)\\
&=&\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} \int_{10}^{t}\diff(\ln(\ln x))\\
&=&\displaystyle \lim_{t\to \infty}\left[ \ln(\ln x)\right]_{x=10}^{x=t}\\
&=&\displaystyle \lim_{t\to \infty}\left(\ln(\ln t)-\ln (\ln 10)\right)\\
&=&\displaystyle \infty,
\end{array}
\]
therefore the integral is divergent (and diverges to $+\infty$).

The function $\frac{1}{x\ln x}$ is decreasing, as for $x>10$, it is the quotient of $1$ by increasing positive functions. $\frac{ 1}{ x\ln x}$ tends to $0$ as $x\to \infty$, and therefore the integral criterion implies that $\sum\limits_{10}^{ \infty} \frac{1 }{n \ln n}$ is divergent.
